The Original

Two Minutes and Thirty-One Seconds

In November 1967, Bob Dylan walked into Columbia Recording Studios in Nashville and recorded "All Along the Watchtower" in a sparse, acoustic arrangement. At 2 minutes and 31 seconds, it was one of the shortest tracks on John Wesley Harding — a cryptic, almost skeletal song built on three chords and a circular structure with no resolution.

That openness turned out to be an invitation. The song's ambiguity — who is the joker? who is the thief? what are they watching for? — left space for every musician who came after to project their own answer.

The Hendrix Moment

September 1968: The Cover That Replaced the Original

Less than a year after Dylan's recording, Jimi Hendrix released his version on Electric Ladyland. It was louder, longer, and electrified — a sonic reimagining so definitive that Dylan himself adopted Hendrix's arrangement for his own live performances.

“It overwhelmed me, really. He had such talent, he could find things inside a song and vigorously develop them. He found things that other people wouldn't think of finding in there.” — Bob Dylan, on Hendrix's version

That moment established a precedent: the definitive version of a Dylan song didn't have to be Dylan's. It was a license that hundreds of artists would claim over the next six decades.

How Long Is a Watchtower?

From One Minute to Nineteen

Dylan's original clocked in at 2 minutes and 31 seconds. But among 456 recordings with duration data, the median cover runs 5.4 minutes — more than double the original. Some artists compress it below two minutes. Others, especially jam bands, stretch it past fifteen.

The Hendrix Watershed

Before and After 1968

Hendrix didn't just cover the song — he reset the template. After 1968, the average Watchtower cover runs 4.7 minutes, nearly double Dylan's original. Artists stopped trying to replicate Dylan's acoustic intimacy and started building on Hendrix's electric foundation.

The song became a vehicle: for guitar solos (Hendrix, Eric Johnson), for arena anthems (U2), for extended jams (Dave Matthews Band, Grateful Dead), for genre experiments (XTC's new wave, Bear McCreary's orchestral arrangement for Battlestar Galactica).

A Song Without a Definitive Version

"All Along the Watchtower" may be the most democratic song in popular music. Dylan wrote it but doesn't own its sound. Hendrix defined it but didn't close it. Each of its 558 recordings is a new answer to the same unanswerable question: what are we watching for?

In that openness — musical, lyrical, structural — lies the reason this song keeps being covered. It isn't finished. It was never meant to be.
