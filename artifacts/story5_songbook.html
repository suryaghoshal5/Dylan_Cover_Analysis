The Folk Rebel as Institution

Bob Dylan spent the 1960s tearing apart the conventions of popular songwriting. He rejected Tin Pan Alley. He went electric to horrify folk purists. He wrote songs that were deliberately too long, too strange, and too personal for the standards repertoire. And yet — here we are.

Adele’s “Make You Feel My Love” reached Spotify popularity 72 — making a 1997 Dylan deep cut one of the most-streamed covers in the entire dataset. 137 artists have now recorded this single song. It has become, by any definition, a standard.

The Songbook Interpreters

These aren’t rock bands paying tribute. They’re jazz vocalists, soul singers, cabaret artists, and crooners who approach Dylan’s compositions the way they’d approach Gershwin or Harold Arlen — as raw material for personal interpretation.

The Songs That Became Standards

Certain Dylan songs attract the songbook treatment more than others. They tend to be melodically strong, harmonically adventurous, and lyrically open enough for reinterpretation — exactly the qualities that define a Great American Songbook entry.

“Don’t Think Twice, It’s All Right” leads with 94 songbook-style recordings across 7 different interpretive artists. Its clean chord changes and singable melody make it a natural jazz standard — even though Dylan wrote it as a breakup letter in 1963.

When Did Dylan Become “Standards”?

The songbook treatment didn’t happen overnight. It followed a clear trajectory: early folk-era respect in the 1960s, a surprising dip in the 1980s, then a sustained revival starting in the 2000s as jazz and soul artists rediscovered Dylan’s harmonic depth.

The 1980s were the dead zone: punk and new wave artists dominated the cover landscape, and jazz/soul interpreters largely abandoned Dylan. The revival began with artists like Cassandra Wilson and Barb Jungr in the late 1990s, who proved that Dylan’s melodies could support sophisticated jazz harmony.

The Spectrum of Interpretation

Not all songbook artists approach Dylan the same way. Country standards singers keep the narrative backbone. Jazz vocalists strip songs to their harmonic essence. Cabaret artists turn them into theatrical confessions.

The Canon Catches Up Slowly

When Adele made a 1997 album track into a wedding staple, she wasn’t doing anything new. She was following a tradition that Nina Simone started in the 1960s, that Cassandra Wilson refined in the 1990s, and that Barb Jungr documented across 83 recordings. The American Songbook already includes Bob Dylan. The liner notes just haven’t been updated yet.
