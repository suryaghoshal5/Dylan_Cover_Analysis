The Nations of Dylan

Each country’s relationship with Dylan tells a different story. France has a single champion who translated an entire catalogue. Germany has a poet who rewrote Dylan in Cologne dialect. Sweden and Poland built whole cottage industries of translation.

Lost & Found in Translation

The most fascinating covers aren’t performances — they’re translations. Artists in six countries have rewritten Dylan’s lyrics in their own languages, forcing choices about what matters most: the literal meaning, the meter, the rhyme, or the feeling.

Hugues Aufray has been translating Dylan into French since 1964 — 176 recordings across 26 songs. His version of “The Times They Are A-Changin’” became “Les temps changent” and entered the French canon on its own terms. Aufray didn’t just translate words; he created a parallel Dylan in French.

Wolfgang Niedecken went further than translation: he rewrote Dylan in Kölsch, the dialect of Cologne. “Like a Rolling Stone” became “Allan wia a Stan” in Viennese dialect (via Wolfgang Ambros), while “I Want You” became “Ich will dich” in standard German. 70 recordings make Niedecken Dylan’s most prolific German interpreter.

What Survives Translation Is the Song

When Hugues Aufray turned “Blowin’ in the Wind” into “Dans le souffle du vent,” he kept the melody and the question. When Wolfgang Niedecken rewrote “I Want You” in Kölsch, he kept the longing. What crosses every border isn’t the English words or the American context. It’s the architecture of feeling that Dylan built into each song.
